# Chapter 1 — Movement 1 — Cold Tea

The shop still held the heat from the forge. Outside, the ridge had already gone blue. Reed set the tongs on the rack and wiped his palm on his thigh. Black grit stayed in the crease of the scar across his knuckle. He rubbed it once, hard, like checking a bolt that always worked loose.

The door chain ticked.

Helen came in without asking, same as last time, coat half-off one shoulder, a paper bag folded under her arm. She stopped just inside the light and took the room in the way she took people—shoulders, hands, the line of a neck held too straight.

“You locked the front,” she said.

“Closes at six.”

“It’s six-forty.”

Reed’s throat tightened before he found an answer. He nodded at the clock above the quench tank. The second hand dragged like it owed him money.

Helen set the bag on the clear end of the bench, away from the scale and the cold chisel. Two paper cups. Steam already dead. She peeled a lid and the smell of tea went thin in the iron air.

“Drink,” she said. “You’ve been on your feet.”

“Shift’s done.”

“That’s when people fall over.”

She stepped inside his reach. Not dramatic—just the small decision of someone who had decided the distance was wrong. Her hand found his forearm, fingers warm through the flannel, trailing once toward the wrist as if measuring grain in wood.

Reed’s shoulders climbed. Jaw set. He did not pull away. Pulling away was a kind of speech, and speech cost more.

“You’re cold,” Helen said. Soft. Certain. Like the diagnosis was already paid for.

“Shop’s warm.”

“You’re not.”

She pressed her palm flatter, thumb at the tendon. His breath snagged high in his chest. For a half-second the room tilted toward her heat, toward the offer in it—sit, eat, let someone carry the weight. Relief walked up like a bill he had not agreed to sign.

He looked past her shoulder at the door. Habit. She followed the look and did not move her hand.

“I still owe you for the spring,” he said. Nouns. Load. Numbers he could stack. “Bracket work. The long one.”

Helen’s chest rose quick. She shook her head once, hair silver-threaded in the forge light.

“You don’t owe me a bracket, Reed.”

“Then I owe you the hours.”

“You owe me nothing.” Her other hand came up, open, almost to his collarbone, then settled on the bench instead—as if she had remembered a rule and hated it. “Sit. Two minutes. Tea’s already cooling.”

He did not sit. He picked up the cup because leaving it would be ruder than drinking. Bitter. Lukewarm. His tongue found the metal taste under the leaves.

Helen watched his throat work. She leaned a fraction closer, voice dropping into that domestic register she used when rooms got stubborn.

“You always do this,” she said. “Someone brings a hand and you start counting.”

“Someone brings a hand, something’s due later.”

“Later is not a person standing here.”

He almost laughed. It snagged and died. Dry. “Later’s patient.”

Helen’s fingers flexed on his sleeve, then gentled—deliberate, like easing a muscle that did not know it was braced. His body answered before he did: a small unclench at the base of the skull, traitorous, gone as soon as he noticed it. He set the cup down harder than he meant. Tea jumped the rim and darkened the wood.

“I’ll finish the gate hinge tomorrow,” he said. “Yours. No charge. That squares it.”

Helen closed her eyes for one blink. When she opened them, the warmth was still there, but tighter at the edges—care with a job attached.

“I didn’t come for a hinge.”

“I know what you came for.”

“Do you.”

He wiped the spill with a rag that had already given up being clean. “To fix what ain’t broken on a schedule.”

Silence stretched. The forge ticked as it cooled. Somewhere in the yard a dog answered another dog and quit.

Helen took her hand back. The air where it had been felt precise and stupid. She folded the empty bag once, twice, small enough for a pocket.

“Drink the rest,” she said. “Then lock up proper. I’ll come by Saturday with food that isn’t cold.”

“You don’t have to—”

“I know.” She was already at the door, coat shrugged on. At the threshold she paused, palm on the frame, looking at him like inventory unfinished. “You’re allowed to put the weight down for a minute, Reed. The world keeps.”

He rubbed the scar with his thumb. Did not answer.

The chain ticked again when she left. He locked it. Checked the side latch. Checked the rack alignment he already knew was true. The second cup sat untouched at the end of the bench, lid still on, cooling into the smell of scale and oil.

Reed drank what was left of his. Bitter. Paid.

Outside, the ridge went black. He turned off the overhead, left the cup where it was, and went out the back with the keys still cutting into his palm.
